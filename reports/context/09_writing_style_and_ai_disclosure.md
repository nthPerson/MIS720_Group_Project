# Writing Style, Voice, and AI Disclosure

This file is the style sheet for the Step 3 deck and the Step 4 report. Use it before drafting prose. None of this is enforced — they are conventions to keep the two deliverables coherent.

## Audience

Per the project guidelines and the Step 2 deck:
- **Mixed-background.** Some attendees and the instructor will be data scientists; others will not be.
- **Step 3:** the audience is in the room and can't backtrack. Define every technical term the first time it appears (ROC AUC, F1, permutation importance).
- **Step 4:** the reader can re-read. A bit more technical density is allowed, but the introduction should still be readable to anyone who's used Spotify.

The instructor's exact framing: *"Try your best to let everyone understand or learn something from your presentation."*

## Voice

- **Confident but honest.** Don't apologize for AUC ≈ 0.87. Don't claim AUC ≈ 0.87 is "very high"; it is "good for this kind of task."
- **First-person plural** ("we trained," "we found") for the methodology and results sections. Acceptable in the academic-paper register the team is targeting.
- **Active voice.** "We trained six workflows" is better than "Six workflows were trained."
- **Concrete numbers.** "Loudness is the strongest" is weaker than "Loudness has a +0.241 point-biserial correlation with the response — the largest among continuous predictors." Use the exact number when it is the point.
- **No hype words.** Avoid "robust," "powerful," "cutting-edge," "state-of-the-art." This is an undergraduate-level course project; over-claiming makes the work look weaker.

## Structural conventions

- **Tables before prose** when comparing models. The eye reads metric tables faster than prose. Then write a paragraph that points at three things in the table.
- **One headline per slide.** A slide with two competing claims is two slides.
- **Audio-vs-genre comparison everywhere.** Whenever you report a number for "the model," report it for both feature sets. The sub-question is the spine — never present a single feature-set result alone.
- **Threshold convention:** ROC AUC and Brier score reported once. Threshold-dependent metrics shown for both default 0.5 and CV-tuned thresholds. See [03_methodology.md](03_methodology.md) §7.
- **Cite the figure file** in the report. "(see Figure X)" is fine; in the .docx the figure number replaces "X."
- **Decimal places:**
  - Probabilities and metrics: 3 decimals (e.g., 0.872).
  - Counts and percentages: integers and 1 decimal point respectively (4,494 tracks; 29.5%).
  - dB values: 2 decimals (−6.77 dB).

## Things to call by their right name

| Wrong | Right |
|---|---|
| "linear regression" (when describing our model) | logistic regression |
| "decision tree" (when describing our model) | random forest |
| "neural network" | not in our model set; use only when discussing future work |
| "the SVM model" | "SVM with RBF kernel" |
| "the database" | "the dataset" or "the working dataset" |
| "training set R-squared" | "CV ROC AUC" or "test ROC AUC" — depending on what is meant |

## Specific phrases to avoid

- **"We achieved" / "we achieved excellent results"** — too marketing-y. Say "we observed" or "we obtained."
- **"Significant"** when you mean "important" or "large." Reserve "significant" for statistically significant (which we don't formally test in this project).
- **"The data shows…"** — agreement: "data show…" but also, the data don't *show*; we *find*. "The data suggest" is fine.
- **"100% accuracy"** as an aspirational target — the instructor explicitly said this is not expected. Don't frame the work against it.

## Slide-deck visual style (Step 3)

The Step 2 discussion deck establishes the visual language. **Match it for continuity.** Specifically:

- **Background:** light off-white for body slides; navy/dark for the title slide.
- **Accent colors:** teal (`#3a8a93`-ish) for primary headings; coral/orange (`#dd6e4a`-ish) for secondary accents like the threshold line and dashed markers. (See the existing R color tokens for exact hex values.)
- **Section tag headers:** small-caps colored tag at the top of each content slide, e.g., "WHAT WE ANALYZED", "REQUIREMENT: BOXPLOTS FOR CATEGORICAL RESPONSE", "POPULARITY THRESHOLD". Use these to signal what kind of evidence each slide is presenting.
- **Big-number callouts** for headline statistics (e.g., "4,494 unique tracks", "29.5% High class") — used on the data overview slide in Step 2 and should reappear on the Step 3 data slide.
- **White callout cards** with a short bullet list for "What we learn" / "Strongest class differences" / "Why this matters" — reuse this pattern.
- **Footer:** "MIS 720 Step 3 Presentation | Team B2" with slide number.

Tables and figures count toward page count in a Word document. Use them aggressively but watch the total.

## When in doubt

- "What does the user need to know to act on this?" — if they don't, cut it.
- "Would the instructor flag this as cargo-culted methodology?" — if yes, explain it more clearly or simplify.
- "Is this number traceable to a script and a CSV?" — if not, don't put it in.
